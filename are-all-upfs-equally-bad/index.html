    Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health? (ZOE, 2025)

    Summary: A ZOE article by Tim Newman, reviewed by Dr Federica Amati, arguing that not all ultra-processed foods carry the same health risk and introducing ZOE's Processed Food Risk Scale as an alternative to the NOVA classification.

    Sources: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md

    Last updated: 2026-04-14

    Overview

    Published April 2025 on zoe.com and reviewed by Federica Amati PhD, MPH, RNutr, this article draws on several large epidemiological studies to argue that the health impact of [[ultra-processed-foods]] depends on the type of UPF, not simply its level of processing (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md).

    It introduces the [[nova-classification]] system, explains its limitations, and presents the [[zoe-processed-food-risk-scale]] as a more nuanced replacement.

    Key argument

    While overall UPF consumption is linked to poorer health outcomes, individual UPF categories behave very differently in research. Some are consistently harmful; others appear neutral or even beneficial (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md).

    Evidence from large studies

    Cardiovascular disease (200,000+ participants, US)

    • Total UPF intake linked to 17% increased cardiovascular disease risk over three decades.
    • Higher-risk UPFs: sugar-sweetened drinks, artificially-sweetened drinks, processed meats.
    • Lower-risk UPFs: bread, cold cereals, yoghurt/dairy desserts, savoury snacks.

    Type 2 diabetes (200,000 participants)

    • Highest UPF consumers had 46% increased type 2 diabetes risk over up to 30 years.
    • Higher-risk UPFs: refined breads, sauces/condiments, artificially/sugar-sweetened drinks, animal-based products, ready-to-eat meals.
    • Lower-risk UPFs: cereals, dark/wholegrain breads, packaged snacks, fruit-based products, yoghurt/dairy desserts.

    Type 2 diabetes (311,892 participants, 2024)

    • Each 10% increase in daily UPFs associated with 17% higher type 2 diabetes risk over 10 years.
    • Higher-risk: savoury snacks, animal-based products, ready meals, sweetened drinks.
    • Lower-risk: breads, biscuits, breakfast cereals, plant-based alternatives.

    Why do UPF types differ in risk?

    The article identifies four factors that explain why some UPFs are more harmful than others (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md):

    1. Chemical additives — colorants, emulsifiers, and preservatives may affect the [[gut-microbiome]].
    2. High energy, sugar, saturated fat, and salt — independently harmful regardless of processing.
    3. Hyperpalatability and energy density — easy to over-consume; 38% of foods in ZOE's PREDICT data were both energy-dense and hyperpalatable.
    4. Displacement of whole foods — eating more UPFs leaves less room for foods that support health.

    Some UPFs, including bread, cereals, and popcorn, can contain [[dietary-fibre]], minerals, and [[polyphenols]], and may not carry the above risks.

    ZOE's practical guidance

    • 80/20 rule: Aim for 80% whole foods, 20% more convenient options.
    • It is "pretty much impossible" to avoid all UPFs; focus on cutting the highest-risk ones consumed most often.
    • See [[zoe-processed-food-risk-scale]] for the full five-tier system.

    Contradiction with earlier source

    The ZOE Gut Health Guide 2024 ([[gut-health-guide-zoe-2024]]) frames UPFs in a broadly negative light without distinguishing between categories. This 2025 article adds important nuance: the same organisation now explicitly states that some UPFs are neutral or beneficial. The practical advice (limit the worst, allow balance) is consistent across both sources.
