    Gut Microbiome

    Summary: The community of trillions of bacteria and other microbes living in the digestive system, which plays a crucial role in overall health.

    Sources: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf

    Last updated: 2026-04-14

    What is the gut microbiome?

    The gut microbiome is the community of trillions of bacteria and other microbes that live in the digestive system (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf). It plays a crucial role in overall health, including digestion, immune function, and nutrient absorption.

    The microbiome lives within the digestive system and is directly influenced by what we eat. A diverse and well-nourished microbiome is associated with better gut health outcomes.

    Why it matters for seniors

    Gut microbiome diversity tends to decrease with age. Supporting the microbiome through diet is therefore particularly relevant for adults over 75. The five steps in the ZOE guide are specifically aimed at helping the microbiome thrive.

    How to support the gut microbiome

    The ZOE guide identifies several dietary approaches that benefit the gut microbiome (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf):

    • Eating a wide variety of plants — see [[plant-diversity]]
    • Consuming adequate [[dietary-fibre]], including prebiotics
    • Eating [[fermented-foods]] containing live beneficial bacteria (probiotic foods)
    • Consuming colourful plants rich in [[polyphenols]]
    • Limiting [[ultra-processed-foods]], which are associated with negative gut health effects

    Key definitions

    • Gut health: The well-being of the digestive system, including the gut lining, microbiome composition, and the ability to digest and absorb nutrients.
    • Prebiotics: Special types of fibre that help beneficial bacteria grow. Found in oats, garlic, onions, leeks, and asparagus.
    • Probiotic foods: Foods containing live microorganisms — typically bacteria or yeasts — that provide health benefits when eaten in the right amounts.
