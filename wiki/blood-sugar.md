# Blood Sugar

**Summary**: How breakfast composition affects blood sugar stability, energy, and hunger throughout the day.

**Sources**: 251030 Breakfast 1761216573-zoe_better_breakfast_guide_us.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-15

---

## What is blood sugar?

Blood sugar (blood glucose) refers to the concentration of glucose in the bloodstream at any given time. When you eat carbohydrates, they are broken down into glucose, which enters the bloodstream. Insulin is then released by the pancreas to move glucose into cells.

## Why stability matters

Blood sugar spikes and crashes can lead to (source: 251030 Breakfast 1761216573-zoe_better_breakfast_guide_us.pdf):

- Low energy and fatigue
- Cravings for sugary or starchy foods
- Difficulty concentrating
- Hunger shortly after eating
- Over-time, repeated spikes may contribute to insulin resistance and increased risk of type 2 diabetes

## The breakfast connection

Breakfast is particularly important for blood sugar management because the composition of your morning meal sets the tone for the rest of the day. ZOE's research shows that blood sugar responses to the same food can vary significantly between individuals (source: 251030 Breakfast 1761216573-zoe_better_breakfast_guide_us.pdf).

## What causes spikes?

- **High refined carbohydrates**: white bread, sugary cereals, orange juice, flavoured yoghurt
   (source: 251030 Breakfast 1761216573-zoe_better_breakfast_guide_us.pdf)
- **Low protein**: without protein to slow absorption, glucose enters the bloodstream quickly
- **Low fibre**: [[dietary-fibre]] slows digestion and absorption; low-fibre meals produce faster sugar entry

## What stabilises blood sugar?

- **Protein**: eggs, yoghurt, cottage cheese, legumes
- **Healthy fats**: avocado, nuts, olive oil
- **Fiber**: oats, seeds, berries, wholegrain bread
- **Fermented foods**: kefir and yoghurt may improve glucose responses (see [[fermented-foods]])
- **Vinegar or sourdough**: acid (acetic acid) slows glucose absorption

## Notes for seniors

For adults over 75, [[blood-sugar]] stability is linked not only to diabetes risk but also to cognitive function, energy levels, and mood. Stable blood sugar throughout the day may reduce fatigue, improve concentration, and support overall wellbeing. This is particularly relevant for seniors who may already have impaired insulin sensitivity.
