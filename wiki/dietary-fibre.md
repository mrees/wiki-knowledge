# Dietary Fibre

**Summary**: A type of carbohydrate found in plant foods that the body cannot digest. It supports a healthy gut microbiome and promotes regular bowel movements.

**Sources**: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf; 251030 Breakfast 1761216573-zoe_better_breakfast_guide_us.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-15

---

## What is dietary fibre?

Dietary fibre is a type of carbohydrate found in plant foods that the body cannot digest (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf). Rather than being absorbed, it passes through the digestive system, where it feeds beneficial gut bacteria and supports bowel regularity.

Fibre is one of the most important nutrients for a healthy [[gut-microbiome]].

## Prebiotics

A special category of fibre called **prebiotics** specifically helps beneficial bacteria grow in the gut (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf). Good sources of prebiotics include:

- Oats
- Garlic
- Onions
- Leeks
- Asparagus

These foods are also featured in the ZOE gut-friendly shopping list and recipes. See [[gut-friendly-recipes]].

## How to increase fibre intake

The ZOE guide recommends these practical steps (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf):

- Make high-fibre swaps (e.g. wholemeal bread instead of white)
- Use pulses and legumes to bulk out dishes
- Batch cook whole grains and store in the fridge
- Keep the skin on vegetables like potatoes and squash

## High-fibre foods

From the ZOE shopping list (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf), good fibre sources include:

- Beans and legumes (chickpeas, lentils, butter beans, black beans)
- Whole grains (oats, brown rice, quinoa, rye, spelt)
- Vegetables (especially with skins on)
- Fruits (particularly berries, pears, apples)
- Nuts and seeds (flaxseeds, chia seeds, pumpkin seeds)

## Resistant starch

Resistant starch is a type of starch that, like fibre, is not digested in the small intestine. Instead, it ferments in the large intestine, feeding beneficial gut bacteria (source: 251030 Breakfast 1761216573-zoe_better_breakfast_guide_us.pdf).

Good sources include:

- Black beans and other legumes
- Cooled cooked potatoes and rice
- Oats

The ZOE Breakfast Hash recipe specifically uses black beans for their resistant starch content. See [[breakfast-recipes]].

## Fibre at breakfast

ZOE data shows that only approximately 6% of people get enough fibre at breakfast — despite this being an important opportunity to start meeting daily fibre needs (source: 251030 Breakfast 1761216573-zoe_better_breakfast_guide_us.pdf). Fibre at breakfast also helps stabilise [[blood-sugar]] by slowing glucose absorption.

## Relevance for seniors

Adequate fibre intake supports bowel regularity, which can be a concern for older adults. It also feeds beneficial gut bacteria, which tend to decline with age.
