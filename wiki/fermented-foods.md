# Fermented Foods

**Summary**: Foods changed by beneficial bacteria or yeasts that break down starches and sugars. They are a key source of probiotic organisms that benefit the gut microbiome.

**Sources**: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## What are fermented foods?

Fermented foods are foods that have been changed by beneficial bacteria or yeasts, which break down their starches and sugars (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf). This process produces live microorganisms — often called probiotics — that can benefit the [[gut-microbiome]] when consumed.

Fermented foods are distinct from [[dietary-fibre]] in that they deliver live bacteria directly, rather than feeding the bacteria already present.

## Examples of fermented foods

From the ZOE guide (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf):

- **Kefir** — a fermented milk drink; also used in recipes such as the green goddess chickpea sandwich
- **Yoghurt** — widely available and easy to incorporate into meals
- **Sauerkraut** — fermented cabbage; can be made at home
- **Kimchi** — fermented vegetables, typically including cabbage and chilli
- **Miso** — fermented soybean paste; used in soups and dressings
- **Kombucha** — fermented tea drink
- **Fermented cheese** — e.g. aged cheddar; a practical option for those unfamiliar with other fermented foods
- **Pickles** — fermented or brined vegetables
- **Tempeh** — fermented soybeans; a good protein source

## Practical tips

The ZOE guide recommends (source: 241101 Gut-Health-Guide-29-12-23.pdf):

- Add a small amount of fermented food to a meal
- Swap half the milk in breakfast with kefir or yoghurt
- Try fermented cheese such as aged cheddar as an easy starting point
- Make your own sauerkraut

## Notes for seniors

Fermented foods are generally well tolerated and easy to include in everyday meals. Yoghurt and kefir in particular are familiar and widely available. Starting with small amounts is advisable if someone is new to fermented foods.
