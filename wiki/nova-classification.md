# NOVA Classification

**Summary**: A food classification system that groups foods into four categories based on the nature and extent of industrial processing. Widely used in research but limited in distinguishing health impact within ultra-processed foods.

**Sources**: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## What is NOVA?

NOVA is a food classification framework, first outlined in 1990, that classifies "all foods according to the nature, extent, and purposes of the industrial processes they undergo" (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md).

It is widely used by researchers studying the relationship between food processing and health.

## The four groups

- **Group 1 — Unprocessed or minimally processed foods**: foods in their natural state, or foods altered only to make them safer or easier to store (e.g. fresh vegetables, plain yoghurt, eggs, fresh meat).
- **Group 2 — Processed culinary ingredients**: substances extracted from Group 1 foods and used in cooking (e.g. oils, butter, flour, sugar, salt).
- **Group 3 — Processed foods**: foods made by combining Groups 1 and 2, typically with salt, sugar, or oil added (e.g. tinned vegetables, cured meats, cheese, freshly baked bread).
- **Group 4 — Ultra-processed foods (UPFs)**: industrial formulations using ingredients rarely found in home kitchens, including artificial colours, emulsifiers, flavourings, and preservatives.

## Limitations of NOVA

The article identifies two key problems with NOVA (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md):

1. **Ambiguity**: Scientists and nutritionists sometimes disagree on how to categorise specific foods.
2. **Blunt instrument for health**: Some Group 4 foods are neutral or even beneficial for health, making NOVA difficult to apply in daily life. The system was designed to classify processing level, not health impact.

Large population studies consistently show that high overall UPF consumption is linked to poorer outcomes — but when UPF types are separated, the picture becomes more complex. See [[are-all-upfs-equally-bad]].

## Alternative: ZOE's Processed Food Risk Scale

ZOE has developed the [[zoe-processed-food-risk-scale]] to address NOVA's limitations. It evaluates foods on energy density, hyperpalatability, energy intake rate, and the type and number of additives — giving a more direct measure of likely health impact.
