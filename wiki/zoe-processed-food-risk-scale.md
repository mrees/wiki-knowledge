# ZOE Processed Food Risk Scale

**Summary**: A five-tier food classification system developed by ZOE that rates processed foods by their likely health impact, accounting for energy density, hyperpalatability, and additive type — rather than processing level alone.

**Sources**: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## What is it?

The ZOE Processed Food Risk Scale is a classification system developed by ZOE to improve on the [[nova-classification]] system (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md). Rather than categorising foods by how much they are processed, it rates them by their likely long-term health impact when consumed regularly.

## What it measures

The scale takes four factors into account (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md):

- **Energy density**: how many calories per gram of food.
- **Energy intake rate**: how quickly those calories are consumed.
- **Hyperpalatability**: whether the food is designed to encourage over-eating.
- **Non-culinary additives**: artificial colours, sweeteners, and preservatives — assessed against rulings by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA).

In ZOE's PREDICT study data (891 people, aged 34–58), energy-dense and hyperpalatable foods were found to make up a large share of typical diets:
- 56% of foods were energy-dense.
- 52% were hyperpalatable.
- 38% were both energy-dense *and* hyperpalatable.

## The five tiers

| Tier | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Unprocessed** | Food in or close to its original form; processing does not affect healthiness. |
| **No Risk** | Minimal processing; poses no risk to health. |
| **Low Risk** | Processing has no or very low impact on health. |
| **Medium Risk** | Processing may make the food less healthy; moderate impact if consumed regularly over the long term. |
| **Highest Risk** | Processing makes the food less healthy; high impact if consumed regularly over the long term. |

## Why it matters for seniors

For adults over 75, the practical value of this scale is in distinguishing which processed foods are worth limiting. Rather than avoiding all convenience foods (which may be helpful for those with reduced cooking capacity), the scale allows a more targeted approach: prioritise cutting **Highest Risk** items such as processed meats and sweetened drinks, while being more relaxed about lower-risk processed options such as wholegrain bread or yoghurt.

## ZOE's 80/20 guidance

ZOE recommends aiming for **80% whole foods and 20% more convenient options** as a realistic long-term balance (source: Are All UPFs Equally Bad For Health_.md).
